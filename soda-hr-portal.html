SODA HR Portal — Employee benefits made self-serve

Built a self-serve HR portal for SME owners and HR teams to onboard employees for medical and flex benefits without relying on brokers or middlemen. The portal lets teams set up policies, create plans, and manage employees end‑to‑end.

Timeline Mar 2025 — Jun 2025 (launch)
Company SODA (DA Group)
Team 2 Designers · 1 PM · 8 Engineers

01

Overview

Problem context

In the TPA market, benefits setup is complex. HR teams often rely on brokers or sales teams to configure policies, onboard employees, and manage changes. Insurance terms and policy rules are not always clear to HR, which slows setup and increases errors.

SODA removes the middleman by letting SME owners and HR teams create their own policies and plans, onboard or deactivate employees, and maintain benefits directly.

Customization challenge

Most companies needed plan flexibility without maintaining multiple policies. SODA enables one policy, one price, and unlimited plans with member groups inside — solving customization at scale.

My role

I owned the HR portal end-to-end — from IA to shipped product. Working directly with the PM and engineering team, I led all design decisions across onboarding, plan setup, employee management, and account controls. The Head of Design worked in parallel on the marketing site and the incoming audience experience, so we had clear ownership across the two surfaces.

02

Process

We started from the existing HR portal thread in DA and rebuilt the flow for self‑serve. Through trial and error, we simplified insurance concepts into guided steps so HR teams could complete setup without broker support.

  • Mapped HR onboarding, deactivation, and plan setup journeys.
  • Prototyped multiple flows to remove broker dependency.
  • Iterated with product, compliance, and engineering to simplify insurance language.
03

Solution

The product we shipped works. HR teams can self-serve — set up policies, create plans, onboard employees, and manage benefits without a broker in the loop. That part landed well. But launching is only half the story.

What we learned quickly is that the harder problem isn't the product — it's reaching the right people. Sales is struggling, not because the tool falls short, but because client acquisition in insurance is deeply relationship-driven. SME owners don't Google for benefits portals. They hear about it from brokers, peers, or through channels we hadn't fully cracked yet. The product was ready before the pipeline was.

As a designer, that shifted my focus. I started working more closely with sales and growth — not just on the portal UX, but on how the product tells its own story. How the onboarding experience itself becomes a sales tool. How the quote flow on the marketing site convinces an HR lead to try self-serve instead of calling a broker. Design became less about features and more about reducing friction at the point of acquisition.

One policy, unlimited plans

Companies no longer maintain multiple policies. One policy, one price — with unlimited plans and member groups inside. This simplification was a key selling point, but it also needed to be immediately obvious to first-time visitors.

Self‑serve onboarding & offboarding

HR teams can onboard or deactivate employees directly. No middleman, no waiting. But making this feel trustworthy for companies used to broker-led processes required careful UX — guided steps, clear confirmation states, and a tone that felt professional, not overwhelming.

Scaling beyond SMEs

The initial product was built for small and mid-size businesses, but we're now evolving the portal to serve larger companies with more complex group structures, tiered plan needs, and higher compliance expectations. The architecture supports it — the experience needs to grow with it.

04

Impact & what's next

Results so far

  • 10k+ employees onboarded after launch.
  • 40+ companies signed up within the first 3 months.
  • HR and SME owners now own their benefits setup end-to-end — no broker in the loop.
  • Customisation no longer requires multiple policies per company.

What's in the pipeline

The portal is live and working. The current focus has shifted to two things: improving user acquisition and fixing experience gaps that surface as more companies onboard.

On the acquisition side, the Head of Design and I are working together — I'm looking at how the portal's own first-run experience can convert more leads (onboarding-as-sales), while the Head of Design is working on the marketing site and the quote flow that brings companies in. The goal is to reduce the drop-off between "heard about SODA" and "first benefits plan created."

On the product side, real usage has surfaced friction points — edge cases in plan setup, gaps in how policy changes communicate downstream, and moments where HR teams lose confidence mid-flow. These improvements are prioritised and actively being designed.

The bigger lesson

Shipping a working product isn't the finish line. In a market where trust is built through brokers and relationships, getting companies to try self-serve is a design problem as much as a sales one. The most impactful work right now is closing the gap between a strong product and the people who need it.

What we learned

  • Product-market fit exists — the gap is in reach, not capability.
  • Insurance acquisition is relationship-first; the product has to earn trust before features matter.
  • Real usage reveals the flows that felt fine in testing but break under edge cases.
  • Designing for growth means working across the full funnel — not just inside the portal.

Active pipeline

  • User acquisition flow improvements
  • First-run experience as a conversion tool
  • Experience fixes from live usage data
  • Scaling to larger enterprise group structures
