Redesigning onboarding to improve identity accuracy at Doctor Anywhere

Redesigned Doctor Anywhere’s onboarding end to end — from fragmented, multi-flow entry points to a single, identity-first experience. The work focused on simplifying entry, improving data accuracy, and delivering a scalable onboarding foundation across B2C, B2B, and direct consult users and as a result we have verified identity data for 1M+ users.

Background & context

Background & Context

When I joined Doctor Anywhere in 2023, the product ecosystem consisted of multiple apps serving different user groups: a core teleconsultation app for public users, multiple white-label apps for employee benefits users tied to insurers, and separate flows and identities across apps.

As the business grew, maintaining many apps became inefficient. Product team chose to consolidate into a single app, making onboarding the mechanism to serve all user groups while meeting MOH compliance. This decision dramatically raised the responsibility of onboarding and it happened incrementally.

The core problem

Onboarding needed to handle multiple responsibilities at once: allow users to sign up and log in, identify user type (B2C, B2B employee benefits, Direct VC users), capture personal details accurately, ensure compliance with MOH requirements, and enable successful downstream experiences (consultations, benefits access). In practice, these responsibilities were closely linked and failures emerged late in the process.

03

Phase 1: Launched to consolidate the onboarding flow

What Phase 1 looked like

In Phase 1, onboarding introduced separate entry points for public and corporate users, corporate sign-up flows requiring insurer-linked information, and KYC completion before first teleconsultation. From a UI perspective, the flows were complete and functional.

Phase 1 map

Separate entry points before pivot

What we observed after launch

After release, issues surfaced across multiple teams. One major signal: of 187K users, about 100K struggled to find or link their membership, revealing how the combined flows were confusing the majority of our base.

  • Onboarding: Public users accidentally using corporate flows; corporate users signing up via public flows; users unable to locate their benefits after login.
  • Patients: Consultations failing because personal details were incorrect; doctors unable to proceed due to mismatched identity information.
  • Compliance/Ops: Stored user data not meeting MOH standards; increased manual intervention and escalations.

Each team experienced the problem differently, but the root cause was the same: we allowed users to progress without being sure who they were. The onboarding flow was optimized for progress, not confidence.

04

Reframing the problem

From speed to confidence

Key problem statement

As a senior product designer, I reframed the problem:

Old focus: How do we help users sign up faster?
New focus: How can the system confidently understand who the user is early in the journey?

How we shifted the problem

We untangled who was arriving: public users, corporate members, and direct consult users. In Phase 1, the flows shipped and usability tests passed, but real usage exposed identity gaps during consults and benefits—issues the tests missed.

The pivot was identity-first: verify early so care and benefits rely on trusted data. Only after identity was confirmed did memberships or corporate access attach; sequence mattered more than polishing UI.

The hardest part was alignment. Working with personal data meant meeting MOH standards with zero trial-and-error. We had to protect the experience while holding the line on data confidence for compliance, operations, and the business.

My role

We identified where onboarding broke down across journeys and mapped failure points. I partnered with product managers, engineering, and compliance to address Singpass constraints and MOH data requirements. I led B2C, B2B, payer, and patient onboarding, driving the identity‑first strategy to ensure the experience was seamless, trusted, and compliant.

Process

Our iterations

Taking ownership of each cycle, I worked closely with product managers, engineering, and compliance to navigate Singpass limitations and MOH data standards. The hardest part wasn’t the design itself, but convincing insurers to accept changes. Insurers are notoriously resistant to shifting processes, so alignment required persistence, framing the pivot as essential for compliance, operations, and long‑term business sustainability. Ultimately, the identity‑first approach became the backbone of onboarding across B2C, B2B, payer, and patient journeys — a strategy built not just on design, but on iteration, negotiation, and stakeholder trust.

06

Phase 2: identity-first onboarding

After carefully captured the user needs and constraints, We started of with Phase 2 which was guided by three principles: reduce early decision-making, verify identity earlier, and separate identity from access.

1. Single sign up entry

One sign-up entry point for all users. No one has to pick B2C, B2B, or Direct VC upfront.

  • One entry for B2C, B2B, and Direct VC users
  • Removes early self-classification

2. Mandatory Identity Verification

Singpass is highly recommended; manual entry stays with clearer guidance. Verification moved earlier.

  • Government-verified personal data
  • Manual entry available with guidance
  • Verification before users use for consultation or benefits

3. Contextual Membership Linking

Only after identity is confirmed do users link employee benefits, connect Direct VC, or continue as public.

  • Benefits and memberships attach post-verification
  • Reduces accidental misuse of corporate flows
  • Improves membership discovery
07

Why Singpass

Why Singpass entered the solution space

We chose Singpass for government-verified data to cut manual entry and satisfy MOH requirements. The design work focused on when to verify, how strongly to require it, and balancing speed with correctness without adding friction.

08

Outcomes

Key outcome summary

Phase 1 consolidated multiple apps into a single app but diluted the quality of personal details. Phase 2 adds verification so the right care reaches the right audience, with onboarding as the crucial moment that sets data quality and shapes the full app experience.

Outcomes (Qualitative)

While metrics evolved over time, Phase 2 resulted in improved accuracy of stored personal data, fewer failed consultations due to identity issues, reduced confusion between user types, better alignment between onboarding and patient workflows, and a more scalable foundation for future user groups.

  • Improved accuracy of stored personal data
  • Fewer failed consultations due to identity issues
  • Reduced confusion between user types
  • Better alignment between onboarding and patient workflows
  • A more scalable foundation for future user groups

Impact

Identity is now established before benefits attach. Consult readiness improved because data quality is higher and visible earlier. Users no longer self-select the wrong path, and corporate/public confusion decreased with one entry and post-verification branching.

So far, 1M+ users have verified via Singpass, the identity-first flow reduced friction and lowered consult drop-offs, and a single, scalable design lets multiple insurers run on one app instead of maintaining separate builds.
